Bryce Eldridge Keeps Impressing, and He Is the Only Future This Giants Team Has

A 21-year-old is hitting .275 with a .368 on-base percentage and a .490 slug on a 42-57 team. He is not a prospect anymore. He is the franchise.

Bay Area Sports Blog Staff · Giants Column

I have watched this team lose in every creative way a baseball team can lose this year. Blown three-run leads. Five-hit afternoons. A road record that reads like a typo. And every single day, no matter how ugly the box score gets, I still turn the game on for one reason, and that reason is a 6-foot-7 kid from Fairfax, Virginia who does not seem to understand that he is supposed to be overmatched. Bryce Eldridge is 21 years old. He has 57 big league games on his resume this season. He is hitting .275 with a .368 on-base percentage, a .490 slugging percentage and an .858 OPS, with 10 home runs, 13 doubles and 30 walks against 57 strikeouts. Read that walk number again. Thirty walks in 200 at-bats from a rookie who spent last September looking completely lost. This is not a hot streak. This is a foundation.

The Numbers Are Not a Fluke, and the Walks Prove It

Every year some rookie gets hot for six weeks and the whole fan base decides he is Willie McCovey. That is not what this is, and the tell is the plate discipline. Batting average moves around. Home run totals cluster. Walk rate is the stat that tells you whether a hitter actually understands what is happening to him, and Eldridge has drawn 30 free passes in 57 games while striking out 57 times. For a 21-year-old, in his first real season, at a position where teams stash free swingers, that ratio is genuinely rare. Pitchers stopped throwing him strikes in June and he did not chase his way out of the league. He took his base, waited for the mistake, and then hit the mistake 450 feet.

The power is not cheap either. On July 5 at Coors Field he hit a ball 458 feet at 106.8 mph off the bat, the longest home run any Giant hit this year. Later that week he had three of the four hardest-hit balls in an 8-2 win, including a 106.1 mph homer. On July 17 in Seattle he took Bryce Miller into right-center for a two-run shot. On July 19, first inning, he hit one 407 feet to dead center off Logan Gilbert to stake this team to a 2-0 lead it inevitably gave away. That is not a guy running into fastballs. That is a guy hitting good pitching, in pitcher-friendly parks, against All-Star arms, at an age when most players in his draft class are still riding buses in Double-A.

June 10 Told You Everything About the Kid

If you need one at-bat, take June 10 against Washington. The Giants had trailed by eight runs. They had clawed back to 10-7. Ninth inning, bases loaded, nobody out, the whole ballpark expecting the same tidy disappointment we have been served all season. Eldridge hit a walk-off grand slam. Eleven to ten. Season saved for a night, one swing, from the youngest player on the roster in the biggest moment of the year to that point.

Big kids are supposed to shrink in those spots. Rookies are supposed to get quick with the bat and roll over the first slider. He did not blink. That is the part you cannot teach and the part that separates a good prospect from a player you build a decade around. We saw it with Posey in 2010. We saw it with Panda in October. This kid has that thing, and he has it at 21.

Where He Came From, and Why It Matters

The Giants took him 16th overall in the 2023 draft out of James Madison High School in Virginia. He came in as a legitimate two-way player, a pitcher and a hitter, and the organization made the correct call in 2024 by telling him to put the glove on first base and hit. He climbed four levels in a season. He got the September call in 2025, went 3 for 28, looked like a 20-year-old drinking from a fire hose, and everybody who watches this team started the usual doomsaying about rushed prospects and ruined confidence.

Then he came back on May 4 of this year and immediately led every qualified National League rookie in average, on-base percentage, slugging and OPS. He did not need a year in Sacramento to figure it out. He needed one offseason and about a month. That is what a real hitter looks like when the light comes on, and it is why the "he is only 21" caveat is not a warning anymore, it is the entire selling point. He is putting up an .858 OPS at an age where his ceiling is still two or three full development years away.

This Team Has No Other Answer, and We Should Act Like It

Look at where we actually are. Forty-two and fifty-seven. Last in the National League West, twenty and a half games behind the Dodgers, twenty games under .500 on the road. The deadline is August 3, and Buster Posey has a decision that should take him about four minutes. There is nothing to defend here. There is no run to make. There is only the question of how many useful pieces you can convert into help that will still be around when Eldridge is 25 and in the middle of his prime.

Because this is the trap the Giants have fallen into over and over since 2016. They find one good young player, they surround him with expensive veterans on the wrong side of thirty, they finish 78-84, and by the time they are honest with themselves the young player is arbitration eligible and the window has closed without ever opening. We cannot do that to this kid. Every wasted at-bat around him, every rental we hold onto out of stubbornness, every month spent pretending fifteen games under .500 is a fixable problem, is a month taken off the front end of the only good thing this organization has produced in a decade.

The Franchise First Baseman We Have Been Missing

Think about how long it has been since first base at Oracle Park meant something. Brandon Belt was the last guy who owned that bag, and he was gone after 2022. Since then it has been a rotating cast of stopgaps, position changes and hope. Now there is a 6-foot-7 left-handed hitter with a .368 on-base percentage standing there at 21 years old, and the job is his for as long as he wants it. That is not a small thing for a franchise that has spent four seasons buying other teams' answers instead of growing its own.

The park is a factor too, and it works in his favor more than people think. Oracle Park has eaten left-handed power for twenty-six years. Triples Alley has turned a hundred would-be home runs into loud outs and long doubles. Eldridge is hitting the ball hard enough, and to the right side of center field with enough carry, that the yard is not going to strangle him the way it strangled others. Thirteen doubles in 57 games in that park is a number that becomes 30 doubles and 30 homers on a normal schedule once he fills out the rest of the way.

What I Actually Want to See the Rest of the Way

Not much, honestly. Just play him. Every day, against lefties, in the tough spots, in the games that do not matter, in September when the stands are half empty and the season is long over. Let him take his 200 more plate appearances. Let him get beat by a good breaking ball in a big spot and figure out the counter. The worst thing this organization could do down the stretch is get cute, rest him into a rhythm-killing rotation, or start protecting him from matchups. He is not a china doll. He is a 21-year-old who has already hit a walk-off grand slam and the longest home run this team has hit all year.

The 2026 Giants are not going anywhere and every one of us knows it. But there is a version of the next five years where this summer gets remembered as the moment the franchise finally found the guy, and the only way that version happens is if the people running the team recognize what is standing at first base every night. He keeps impressing. He keeps answering. He is the future, and if we are being honest about it, he is already the present too.
