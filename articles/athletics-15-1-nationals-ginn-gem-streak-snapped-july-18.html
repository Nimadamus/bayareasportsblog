The A's Just Beat Somebody 15-1, and I Checked the Box Score Three Times to Be Sure

One night after the most embarrassing loss of the season, JT Ginn took a no-hitter into the seventh, three different guys went deep, and the 10-game losing streak is finally, mercifully dead.

Bay Area Sports Blog Staff · A's Column

Twenty-four hours. That is how long it took this team to go from the single most humiliating night of its season to the most cathartic one. On Friday the Nationals hung 23 runs on the A's in their own building, a 23-4 loss so ugly I genuinely considered not writing about this team again until August. On Saturday, the same A's, the same building, the same opponent, and a 15-1 annihilation that snapped a 10-game losing streak and reminded everybody in West Sacramento that this roster does, in fact, contain professional baseball players. Baseball is a ridiculous sport. Never let anyone tell you otherwise.

Let's start with the man who deserves the game ball, because JT Ginn did not just pitch well, he pitched the kind of game that saves a clubhouse. One night after the pitching staff got lit up for 23 runs, Ginn walked out there and carried a no-hitter into the seventh inning. His final line: six and a third innings, one hit, zero runs, seven strikeouts. Against a Washington lineup that had spent the previous evening treating Sutter Health Park like a batting cage, Ginn gave them absolutely nothing. You want to know how a team stops a 10-game losing streak? It is not a players-only meeting or a hot-dog promotion. It is a starting pitcher standing on the mound and saying none of you are reaching base tonight, and meaning it into the seventh.

And this time the offense did not make him do it alone. The A's put up five runs in the first inning, and I need you to appreciate what that felt like after the last two weeks. Five runs, first inning, game functionally over before the beer lines died down. Jacob Wilson got it started with a homer in the first, the shortstop setting the tone before some fans had found their seats, and from there the hits just kept coming. Thirteen of them by night's end, fifteen runs total, and not a single error in the field. After ten straight games of finding brand-new ways to lose, they spent one entire evening doing everything right, and honestly, watching it felt like exhaling for the first time since the break.

The power kept rolling all night. Tyler Soderstrom crushed a two-run shot in the sixth. Carlos Cortes, who has quietly been one of the best stories on this team while hitting .350 since his call-up, added a solo homer in the seventh, the same inning the A's decided one touchdown was not enough and hung a six-spot on the Washington bullpen. Shea Langeliers, fresh off starting the All-Star Game, ripped a three-RBI double. Top to bottom, everybody ate. Washington starter Zack Littell wore eight of the fifteen runs and took the loss, and there is something deeply satisfying about watching the team that scored 23 on you go home with one run on four hits.

Now for the part where I keep my feet on the ground, because I have watched this team all year and I know better. The A's are 42-56. One 15-1 win does not un-lose the ten games before it, and it does not change the fact that they arrived at this weekend in fourth place in the AL West with a roster that still cannot string together three competent weeks. The same team that scored 15 tonight scored four runs combined across multiple games during the streak. This franchise's whole 2026 identity is whiplash: they handled the Dodgers 7-1 at the start of the month, then lost ten straight; they got outscored 23-4, then won 15-1. Whatever this team is, consistent is not the word.

But here is why tonight still matters, and why I am letting myself enjoy it. Losing streaks are heavy. Ten games of it, capped by a 23-4 beating in front of your own fans, is the kind of stretch that breaks young teams and gets people fired. The response options were: roll over and lose 6-2 in front of 11,000 people, or come out and stomp somebody. They stomped somebody. The kids led it, too. Wilson, Soderstrom, Cortes, Langeliers, Ginn, that is the core this whole rebuild is riding on, and on Saturday night every single one of them showed up in the same game. That is the version of this team we were promised back in April. It exists. We have now seen it.

So no, I am not going to pretend one blowout win fixes a 42-56 season, and I am not going to pretend the whiplash will not swing back the other way by Tuesday. But the streak is dead, the kids all ate, Ginn was brilliant, and the Nationals got sent home with a one-run reminder that baseball's memory is exactly one day long. After the two weeks this fan base just sat through, I will take 15-1 and I will not apologize for smiling about it. Rubber game Sunday. Win the series and we can talk about whether this team has a pulse after all.
