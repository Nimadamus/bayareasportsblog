The Giants Blew a 3-0 Lead to the Mariners, and I Am Sick of Writing This Column

Logan Webb was carving. The Mariners had one hit through six. Then one swing from a kid named Cole Young erased everything, and Seattle walked it off 4-3 in ten. Again. It happened again.

Bay Area Sports Blog Staff · Giants October Watch

I want you to sit with this one for a second, because I had to. Saturday night in Seattle, the Giants led the Mariners 3-0 in the seventh inning. Logan Webb was carving through one of the better lineups in the American League like it was a spring training split squad. Seattle had managed almost nothing against him all night. Rafael Devers and Willy Adames had both gone deep. Twenty-four hours after a 7-0 shutout in the same building, we were about to take the first two games of a road series from a division contender, and for once I let myself believe this team had actually turned into something. And then, because these are the Giants and because I am apparently a fool who never learns, it all went in the toilet. Final: Mariners 4, Giants 3, in ten innings. Another lead, blown. Another winnable game, handed away. I am so tired of writing this exact column.

Let me walk you through the crime scene, because the details matter. The Giants scratched out a run in the third when Bryce Eldridge singled and a Seattle error in center let a run come home. Then in the sixth, the two bats we are paying to be the heart of this thing actually did their jobs. Devers ran into one for a solo homer, and Adames followed with a solo shot of his own right behind him. 3-0. On the road. Against Bryan Woo, who is nobody's idea of a soft touch. That is exactly the kind of game a real team wins going away. That is the formula: your ace deals, your stars slug, your bullpen closes the door. Except this bullpen does not close doors. It takes them off the hinges and invites the other team to walk through.

Here is the number that should make you want to throw something: the Mariners had THREE hits in this game. Three. You cannot lose a baseball game when you give up three hits. It should be mathematically impossible. But the Giants found a way, because one of those three hits was a 405-foot missile off the bat of Cole Young in the seventh, a three-run homer to right-center with Randy Arozarena and Josh Naylor aboard, and just like that a 3-0 lead was a 3-3 tie. One pitch. One swing from a second-year infielder, and everything Webb built over six and two thirds innings was ash. Webb's final line was six and two thirds, two hits, three runs, one walk, five strikeouts, and if you are wondering how a man allows two hits and three runs and takes the loss column hit for it, congratulations, you have discovered the 2026 San Francisco Giants, where the math is always broken and the lead is never safe.

And of course it went to extras, because these games always go to extras, and of course it ended the way it ended. Tenth inning, free runner on second, and Julio Rodriguez did the easiest thing in the world: he lifted a fly ball to left, Victor Robles trotted home, and 43,485 people at T-Mobile Park went home happy while our guys walked off the field with that same thousand-yard stare I have seen all season. A sacrifice fly. We lost on a sacrifice fly. The Mariners did not even need a hit to win it. That is the most Giants ending imaginable to the most Giants game imaginable.

I keep coming back to Webb, because this is the part that actually makes me angry instead of just numb. This man has been the one constant on this roster for years. He takes the ball every fifth day, he pitches deep into games, he never complains, and this team wastes him over and over and over. Six and two thirds innings of three-hit baseball on the road against a playoff-caliber lineup should be an automatic win. Instead he is sitting in the clubhouse tonight with nothing to show for it, again, because the margin for error on this team is zero. One mistake pitch in the seventh inning and the whole night dies. No lead is big enough. No start is good enough. If I were Webb's agent I would be forwarding highlights of this game to every contender in baseball with a note that says get him out of here before this franchise grinds what is left of his prime into dust.

And I know what some of you are going to say, because I have read the replies before. It is one game. They won 7-0 yesterday. They are playing better since the break. Fine. All true. They came into tonight having won four of six, Friday was legitimately fun, and at 42-56 nobody sane thought this season was going anywhere anyway. But that is exactly why this one stings. The measure of whether this team is actually improving or just rearranging deck chairs is simple: can you hold a late lead against a good team on the road? Tonight was the test. Three-run lead, your ace on the mound, eight outs to get. They failed it. Again. This is the same disease that killed them in July when Caleb Kilian coughed up a two-out lead to the worst team in baseball, the same disease that has been eating this bullpen alive all year. Different arm, same funeral.

Tony Vitello talked coming out of the break about a clean slate and a fresh start, and for about thirty hours I bought it. Friday night looked like a new team. Saturday night looked like the same old one wearing Friday's clothes. A rookie manager learning on the job, a bullpen with no trustworthy leverage arm, and a front office that has watched this exact failure repeat for three and a half months without doing a single thing about it. The trade deadline is August 3. If Buster Posey watches this game and does not come away understanding that this roster needs real late-inning pitching, or needs to be sold off for parts entirely, then I do not know what he is watching.

So here is where we are. The Giants are 42-56. They have now split the first two in Seattle, and there is one more game in this series for them to decide which team shows up. I will watch it, because I always watch, because being a fan of this team is a documented medical condition at this point. But do not ask me to believe anymore. Believing is what I did in the seventh inning tonight, right up until Cole Young swung. Three hits. We gave up three hits and lost. Somewhere in the baseball afterlife, the ghosts of every Giants bullpen collapse going back decades looked at this box score and nodded in recognition. Welcome to the family, kid. There is always room for one more.
