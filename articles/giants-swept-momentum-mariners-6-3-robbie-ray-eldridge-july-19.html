The Giants Lost the Series in Seattle, and the Fresh Start Lasted Exactly One Game

Bryce Eldridge hit a ball 407 feet. We led 2-0 after one inning. Then Robbie Ray was gone after four, the offense fell asleep for eight innings, and Seattle took it 6-3 and took the series. Friday feels like it happened to a different team.

Bay Area Sports Blog Staff · Giants October Watch

You know what the worst part of being a Giants fan in 2026 is? It is not the losing. I have made my peace with the losing. It is the little sip of hope they pour you right before they snatch the glass away. Sunday afternoon at T-Mobile Park, Bryce Eldridge, our nineteen-year-old franchise cornerstone, our one true reason to watch this team, launched a baseball 407 feet to dead center in the first inning. Heliot Ramos followed with an RBI single. Two batters in, 2-0 Giants, on the road, against Logan Gilbert, with a series win sitting right there. And then, for the next eight innings, this offense produced almost nothing, the pitching leaked runs in four separate innings, and the Mariners walked away with a 6-3 win and the series. After Friday's 7-0 statement and Saturday's gut-punch, we needed exactly one win to leave Seattle feeling like a real baseball team. We could not do it. Of course we could not do it.

Let us start with the offense, because five hits is not an offense, it is a rumor of one. Five. Gilbert struck out TEN Giants over six innings and walked one, and after the first inning he was playing with his food. Eldridge got him for the homer, Ramos cashed the run in, and then the entire lineup collectively decided the job was done. Luis Arraez doubled home a run in the fifth to make it 3-2, and I genuinely thought, here we go, they are answering, this is what competent teams do. That was the last run the San Francisco Giants scored. From the sixth inning on, against Seattle's bullpen, this team put up nothing. Two errors in the field for good measure, because when the bats quit, why should the gloves keep working?

Now Robbie Ray. I want to be fair to the man, because his line looks weirder than it played: four innings, seven hits, one earned run, six strikeouts, 86 pitches. One earned run! Sounds fine, right? Except he needed 86 bullets to get twelve outs because Seattle put seven balls in play for hits and made him grind through traffic every single inning. A veteran making what he makes, in a game your club desperately needs, has to give you more than four innings. Seattle tied it against him with two in the fourth, helped along by a defense that kicked the ball around for two errors on the day, and when he walked off with the bases loaded in the fifth you knew how this movie ends, because we have all seen it forty times this year. Seattle tied it right back up in the bottom of that fifth, Luke Raley put them ahead for good with a 386-foot shot in the sixth, and Cole Young, yes, THAT Cole Young, the same kid who broke our hearts with the three-run bomb on Saturday, hit ANOTHER homer in the seventh, a two-run shot to right that turned 4-3 into 6-3 and turned my Sunday into a chore. One prospect on their side did more damage in two days than half our roster has done in two weeks.

Here is the zoomed-out picture, and it is ugly. The Giants are 42-57. They are 20-32 on the road. They came out of the All-Star break with all that fresh-start talk, hung seven on Seattle in the opener, and then dropped two straight winnable games to hand the series to a Mariners team that just got back to .500 at 50-50 on our dime. Friday night I wrote that the second half might be different. Saturday they blew a 3-0 lead on three hits. Sunday they led 2-0 and lost by three. That is not a fresh start. That is the same team with a shower and a clean uniform.

The Eldridge part deserves its own paragraph, because it is the only thing worth keeping from this weekend. The kid homered Friday. The kid homered Sunday, 407 feet, first inning, off an All-Star caliber right-hander, at nineteen years old. He is not the future anymore, he is the present, and the present is being wasted surrounded by a lineup that manages five hits on a Sunday and a pitching staff that cannot hold a two-run lead for even one inning. Buster Posey, the deadline is August 3. Every at-bat this team wastes around Eldridge is a crime against the one asset we have. Sell the expiring pieces, get this kid some help that will still be here in 2028, and stop pretending 42-57 is anything other than what it is.

And spare me the silver linings about Ray's strikeouts or the two-run first. Good teams finish road trips with kill shots. This team got a 2-0 lead handed to it by a teenager and responded by going quietly for eight innings in front of 36,204 people who mostly showed up to watch their own team climb back to .500. The Mariners are trending up. The Giants are fifteen games under and trending nowhere. I will be back at it tomorrow because that is the sickness, but today, do not ask me to be reasonable. Five hits. Four innings from the veteran starter. A series loss to a .500 team after leading in all three games. Same team. Same story. Same disgusted columnist, filing the same obituary with a different date on it.
